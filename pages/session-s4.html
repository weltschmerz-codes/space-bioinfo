3.1 The Spaceflight Environment

The International Space Station (ISS) orbits Earth at approximately 408 km altitude, completing one orbit every 90 minutes. Crew members aboard the ISS are simultaneously exposed to multiple physical stressors that have no equivalent on Earth's surface. Understanding these stressors and their biological effects is essential before analyzing spaceflight omics data — because the data will reflect responses to all of them simultaneously.

3.1.1 Microgravity Effects

In low Earth orbit, astronauts experience microgravity (approximately 10⁻⁶ g). At the cellular level, microgravity removes gravity-dependent mechanical signals that cells normally use to maintain structure and function. Effects include: skeletal muscle atrophy (1–2% of muscle mass lost per month without countermeasures), bone density loss (1–2% per month in weight-bearing bones), fluid redistribution toward the upper body (cephalad fluid shift), and immune dysregulation.

At the molecular level, transcriptomics studies consistently show dysregulation of genes involved in cytoskeletal organization, mechanotransduction, and myogenesis in response to microgravity.

3.1.2 Space Radiation

The ISS is partially shielded by Earth's magnetic field but crew members still receive approximately 0.5–1 mSv per day (compared to ~3 mSv per year on Earth's surface). This radiation includes galactic cosmic rays (GCRs) — high-energy particles from outside our solar system that can penetrate spacecraft shielding — and solar energetic particles (SEPs) from solar flares. At the molecular level, space radiation causes DNA double-strand breaks, oxidative stress, and activates DNA damage repair pathways detectable by transcriptomics.

3.3 The NASA Twin Study

The most comprehensive omics study of human spaceflight to date is the NASA Twin Study (2015–2016), which compared astronaut Scott Kelly — who spent 340 days aboard the ISS — to his identical twin brother Mark Kelly, who remained on Earth as a ground control. Because the twins share the same genome, any differences in omics profiles could be attributed to the spaceflight environment rather than genetic variation.

Omics data collected included telomere length and telomerase activity, whole-genome sequencing (somatic mutations), epigenomics, transcriptomics (RNA-seq of peripheral blood mononuclear cells), proteomics, metabolomics, and microbiome analysis.

Key Findings:

  • Telomere elongation during spaceflight: counterintuitively, telomeres lengthened during flight but rapidly shortened upon return
  • Gene expression changes: ~91% of gene expression changes returned to preflight levels within 6 months, but ~9% showed persistent changes
  • Cognitive performance decline: cognitive scores were lower after spaceflight, persisting for months post-flight
  • Epigenetic changes: DNA methylation patterns changed significantly, particularly in immune-related genes
  • Microbiome shifts: gut microbiome composition changed during flight and returned toward baseline upon landing
💡 Why Multi-Omics?

If only one omics layer had been measured, many of the most important findings would have been missed. For example: telomere changes would be invisible in a transcriptomics-only study. The integration of multiple omics layers allows researchers to distinguish which changes are at the DNA level, the RNA level, the protein level, and whether they are consistent across layers.

3.4 Experimental Design Challenges

  • Sample size limitations: The ISS typically hosts only 6 astronauts simultaneously. A spaceflight experiment might have n=3–6 biological replicates per group — leading to reduced statistical power and higher false discovery rates.
  • Ground control design: Defining the appropriate control condition is non-trivial. The gold standard is a 1g centrifuge aboard the ISS, which provides normal gravity in space, isolating microgravity effects specifically.
  • Confounding variables: Because spaceflight simultaneously presents microgravity, radiation, stress, dietary changes, and circadian disruption, it is often impossible to attribute molecular changes to a single cause.

Session Summary

  • Spaceflight presents multiple simultaneous biological stressors: microgravity, radiation, psychological stress, altered nutrition, and microbiome disruption
  • These stressors have measurable molecular correlates detectable through omics approaches
  • The NASA Twin Study demonstrated that ~9% of gene expression changes persist beyond 6 months post-flight
  • Small sample sizes, confounding variables, and ground control design are key experimental challenges
  • Multi-omics integration is essential because no single layer captures the full biological picture
