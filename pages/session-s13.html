12.1 What is Network Analysis?

Differential expression analysis (Sessions 9–10) gives us a list of genes that change significantly between conditions. But a list of genes tells us relatively little on its own — genes do not act in isolation. They encode proteins that form physical complexes, regulate each other's transcription, and participate in multi-step biochemical pathways. Network analysis treats this web of interactions as a graph and uses mathematical measures to identify which proteins are most central to the network's structure and function.

In a protein–protein interaction (PPI) network, each node is a protein and each edge is a known or predicted interaction between two proteins. Highly connected nodes — called hub genes — correspond to proteins that participate in many interactions, making them likely to be essential to cellular function. Disrupting a hub gene has outsized consequences compared to disrupting a peripheral node, which is why hubs are particularly interesting targets for understanding disease, drug resistance, and — in our context — the physiological response to spaceflight.

💡 Why Network Analysis After DEG Analysis?

Multi-omics data from spaceflight are massive and complex in nature. One way of uncovering important information is analyzing them as networks. While DESeq2 tells you which genes change, network analysis tells you which of those changes are most biologically consequential — by revealing which altered proteins sit at the center of the molecular interaction landscape.

12.1.2 The Reference Paper

This session is structured around a study published by researchers from the Philippine Space Agency: "Uncovering Hub Genes in Short Duration Spaceflight" (Gasatan, Improso & Macalalad, PhilSA). The paper analyzes transcriptomic data from civilian astronauts in the SpaceX Inspiration4 (I4) mission — the first all-civilian orbital spaceflight — to identify hub genes affected by short-duration space exposure.

📄 Reference Gasatan M. J. B., Improso W. D. G., & Macalalad E. P. (2025). Uncovering Hub Genes in Short Duration Spaceflight. Philippine Space Agency. Available at: philsa.gov.ph/research/uncovering-hub-genes-in-short-duration-spaceflight/

The I4 mission carried four crew members on a 3-day orbital flight with no prior spaceflight experience. Their blood transcriptomes were sequenced before and after the mission, capturing the rapid molecular response to microgravity and cosmic radiation in a naive human population — making it a uniquely clean dataset for studying the immediate effects of the space environment.

12.3 From DEGs to a PPI Network

The I4 mission transcriptomic data is available on OSDR at OSD-569. The DEG dataset was derived from direct RNA sequencing of astronaut blood, as reported by Grigorev et al. (2024), yielding 437 differentially expressed genes across timepoints before and after the 3-day flight. These gene IDs are in Ensembl format (ENSG...) — but STRING and Cytoscape work with protein identifiers, so the first step is a conversion.

12.3.1 ID Mapping in UniProt

Navigate to uniprot.org/id-mapping/. Paste your Ensembl gene ID list, set From: Ensembl and To: UniProtKB/Swiss-Prot, and run the mapping. Only protein-coding genes have SwissProt entries — non-coding RNAs, pseudogenes, and lncRNAs will not map and are automatically excluded.

💡 Swiss-Prot vs TrEMBL

UniProt has two sections: Swiss-Prot (manually reviewed, high quality) and TrEMBL (automatically annotated). Always map to Swiss-Prot for PPI network construction — this ensures your protein entries have been manually curated for accuracy. The PhilSA paper filtered 437 DEGs down to 332 coding genes this way, discarding 105 non-coding genes.

12.3.2 Constructing the PPI Network in STRING

With your 332 UniProt IDs ready, go to string-db.org and use Multiple Proteins → Search. Paste your protein list and set the organism to Homo sapiens. Apply the following three filters before exporting:

FilterValue UsedWhy It Matters
OrganismHomo sapiensEnsures human-specific interaction data is used; prevents cross-species predictions
Minimum interaction scoreHigh confidence (0.700)Removes low-confidence predicted interactions; retains only well-supported edges
FDR stringency0.05Controls the false positive rate in the network; limits inclusion of spurious interactions

After applying filters, remove any disconnected nodes (proteins with no edges under your score threshold) — these cannot contribute to hub analysis. Export the network using Exports → TSV format and then open it in Cytoscape via File → Import → Network from File.

💡 The I4 PPI Network at a Glance

After applying all filters, the PhilSA study's network retained 207 nodes and 1,568 edges from the original 332 coding genes. The high edge density (average ~15 edges per node) reflects the highly interconnected nature of blood transcriptome proteins, many of which are part of broad regulatory complexes.

12.4 Network Visualization in Cytoscape

Cytoscape is a desktop application for network visualization and analysis. Once you import your STRING network, the raw layout will appear as an unstructured "hairball" — visually dense and difficult to interpret. Plugins and layout algorithms transform this into a readable, publication-quality figure.

12.4.1 Essential Cytoscape Plugins

Install these three plugins from Apps → App Manager inside Cytoscape:

PluginPurpose
stringAppImports STRING networks directly into Cytoscape, preserving interaction scores and edge annotations. Also enables enrichment queries within Cytoscape.
cytoHubbaCalculates centrality measures (degree, betweenness, closeness, and others) for all nodes and ranks them. Used to identify hub genes in Section 12.5.
yFiles Layout AlgorithmsProvides advanced graph layout algorithms. The yFiles Circular Layout is particularly useful for visualizing hub gene subnetworks cleanly.
💡 Clean Visualization Tip — Display Names

When STRING imports a network, node labels default to UniProt accession numbers (e.g. P62979), which are not readable in a figure. Under the Style tab in Cytoscape, click the Label mapping, set the Column to display name, and the nodes will show human gene symbols (e.g. UBA52) instead — essential for any figure you plan to publish or present.

12.5 Hub Gene Identification with cytoHubba

Hub genes are highly connected nodes in the PPI network. Because they participate in more interactions, they are more likely to be involved in essential biological processes — disrupting them has broader consequences than disrupting peripheral nodes. The cytoHubba plugin computes multiple centrality metrics simultaneously; we use two complementary measures to identify robust hubs.

12.5.1 Degree Centrality

Degree centrality is the simplest measure: it is simply the number of edges (interactions) a node has. A protein with a high degree interacts with many other proteins in the network, suggesting it participates in diverse biological processes or acts as a scaffold in a large complex.

Degree(v) = number of edges incident to node v

12.5.2 Betweenness Centrality

Betweenness centrality measures how often a node lies on the shortest path between any two other nodes. A protein with high betweenness acts as a critical bridge — information or signals passing through the network must flow through it. Removing a high-betweenness node can fragment the network into disconnected components, which is why these nodes are particularly important biologically.

Betweenness(v) = Σ (σ_st(v) / σ_st)  for all pairs s≠v≠t

# where σ_st = number of shortest paths from s to t
#       σ_st(v) = number of those paths passing through v

# High-betweenness nodes in the I4 PPI network:
# UBA52 → Score 5733.13 (Rank 1)
# RPS15 → Score 2826.23 (Rank 7)
# FAU   → Score 2224.53 (Rank 10)
💡 Why Use Two Measures?

Degree captures local connectivity (how many direct neighbors a node has), while betweenness captures global network position (how critical a node is for connecting distant parts of the network). A gene that ranks highly in both measures is a more confident hub than one that excels in only one. The PhilSA paper selected genes in the top 30 of both measures as the final hub gene set.

🔬 Biological Insight — The Ribosomal Signature

All eight hub genes encode ribosomal proteins (RPs) — components of either the 40S small subunit (RPS genes) or 60S large subunit (RPL35, UBA52, FAU). This convergence on a single functional category is striking and biologically meaningful: it suggests that short-duration spaceflight specifically disrupts the protein synthesis machinery at a molecular network level, beyond what a simple DEG count reveals.

12.6 Functional Enrichment Analysis (FEA)

With 8 hub genes identified, the next step is to ask: what biological functions are overrepresented in this list compared to what we would expect by chance? Functional enrichment analysis (FEA) answers this by testing whether the hub gene list contains significantly more genes associated with specific GO terms or KEGG pathways than a random sample of the same size.

The analysis is performed using ShinyGO v0.82. Paste the 8 hub gene symbols into the input box, set organism to Homo sapiens, and use an FDR threshold of 0.05. Only terms with FDR < 0.05 are considered significant.

12.6.1 Gene Ontology (GO) Results

Gene Ontology classifies genes along three orthogonal axes. The I4 hub genes produced significant enrichment in all three categories:

GO CategorySignificant TermsInterpretation
Biological Process (BP)Translation · Peptide metabolic process · Peptide biosynthetic process · Cytoplasmic translation · Amide biosynthetic processHub genes collectively drive the production of proteins from mRNA — the core translation machinery is disrupted
Cellular Component (CC)Ribosome · Ribosomal subunit · Cytosolic ribosome · Small ribosomal subunit · Cytosolic small ribosomal subunitHub genes physically localize to the ribosome complex, especially the 40S small subunit

12.6.2 KEGG Pathway Results

The KEGG pathway analysis identified two significantly enriched pathways: Ribosome and COVID-19. The COVID-19 pathway co-enrichment is a known artifact — the SARS-CoV-2 virus hijacks host ribosomes for viral protein production, so ribosomal proteins appear in the COVID-19 pathway annotation. This result does not imply a biological connection to COVID-19 in the spaceflight context and is not interpreted further.

The dominant KEGG signal — the Ribosome pathway — powerfully corroborates the GO results: the hub genes of the I4 spaceflight transcriptome are almost exclusively ribosomal in nature.

⚠️ Interpreting COVID-19 in KEGG Enrichment Results

When you run FEA on ribosomal protein gene lists, COVID-19 will frequently appear as a significant KEGG pathway. This is because COVID-19 pathway annotations include many host ribosomal proteins that the virus co-opts. Always check the gene members of an enriched pathway before interpreting it biologically — the presence of a pathway name does not guarantee a direct biological connection to the experimental condition.

12.7 Cross-Validation with SOMA

A finding is only as strong as its replication. The PhilSA study validated the 8 hub genes by cross-referencing their expression across three independent spaceflight datasets in the Space Omics and Medical Atlas (SOMA) — a publicly available international astronaut biobank.

DatasetMissionDurationHub Gene Status
I4 DataSpaceX Inspiration43 daysSource dataset — all 8 hubs identified here
NASA Twins StudyISS (OSD-530)340 daysAll 8 hub genes consistently differentially expressed
JAXA Cell-Free RNAISS JAXA (OSD-530)120 daysAll 8 hub genes consistently differentially expressed

The fact that the same 8 ribosomal protein hub genes are differentially expressed across missions spanning 3 to 340 days — covering civilian and professional astronauts, different mission profiles, and different measurement technologies — is a remarkably consistent signal. It suggests that ribosomal protein dysregulation is a universal and early-onset response to the space environment, not a peculiarity of any single mission.

💡 Biological Significance — Ribosome Biogenesis and Spaceflight
  • Ribosome biogenesis disruption is caused by altered expression of ribosomal components and is linked to cell cycle arrest, senescence, and apoptosis
  • This interference is associated with cancer and age-related degenerative diseases — both conditions already known to have elevated risk in spaceflight
  • The space environment is increasingly recognized as an accelerator of biological ageing; ribosomal protein dysregulation may be one molecular mechanism underlying this observation
  • Under cellular stress, decreased ribosome activity leads to reduced protein synthesis and growth arrest — suggesting spaceflight may trigger a conserved stress-response program at the translational level

Session Summary

  • PPI network analysis extends DEG analysis by revealing which differentially expressed proteins occupy central positions in the molecular interaction landscape — revealing hub genes that may be missed by fold-change ranking alone
  • The pipeline flows: DEGs → UniProt ID mapping (coding only) → STRING PPI (confidence ≥ 0.7, FDR < 0.05) → Cytoscape (with stringApp, cytoHubba, yFiles) → hub gene ranking → FEA (ShinyGO) → cross-validation (SOMA)
  • Degree centrality counts direct interactions; betweenness centrality measures global bridge importance — intersecting the top 30 of both measures gives robust, reproducible hub genes
  • The 8 hub genes of the Inspiration4 mission (UBA52, FAU, RACK1, RPS2, RPS11, RPS14, RPS15, RPL35) all encode ribosomal proteins, revealing a consistent spaceflight signature of translational disruption
  • FEA with ShinyGO confirmed ribosome-related GO and KEGG terms as significantly enriched; COVID-19 KEGG co-enrichment is a known artifact of ribosomal protein gene sets and should not be over-interpreted
  • SOMA cross-validation showed the same 8 hub genes are differentially expressed across 3-day, 120-day, and 340-day missions, suggesting ribosomal protein dysregulation is a universal and early-onset response to spaceflight
