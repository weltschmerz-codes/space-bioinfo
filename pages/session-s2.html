1.1 What Is Bioinformatics?

Bioinformatics is an interdisciplinary field that combines biology, computer science, mathematics, and statistics to develop methods and tools for understanding biological data. At its core, bioinformatics answers a simple but profound question: how do we make sense of the enormous amounts of data that modern biology generates?

To put this in perspective: a single human genome contains approximately 3.2 billion base pairs. Sequencing that genome costs less than a thousand dollars today and can be done in a matter of days. But generating the data is only the beginning; interpreting it requires sophisticated algorithms, large databases, and advanced statistical frameworks. This is where bioinformatics becomes indispensable.

Bioinformatics sits at the intersection of several disciplines:

  • Biology: provides the scientific questions and biological context
  • Computer Science: provides algorithms, software, and data structures
  • Statistics & Mathematics: provides the analytical frameworks
  • Chemistry & Biochemistry: provides mechanistic understanding

1.1.1 A Working Definition

The National Institutes of Health (NIH) defines bioinformatics as the research, development, or application of computational tools and approaches for expanding the use of biological, medical, behavioral, or health data, including those used to acquire, store, organize, archive, analyze, or visualize such data.

In these lecture notes, the focus is on bioinformatics applied to molecular biology, specifically in the context of how organisms respond to the extreme environment of spaceflight.

1.2 The Central Dogma Revisited

Before diving into bioinformatics tools, we need a clear picture of the biological information flow that bioinformatics aims to analyze. The central dogma of molecular biology, originally proposed by Francis Crick in 1958, describes how genetic information flows within a biological system:

LevelDescription
DNA → RNA (Transcription)Genetic sequence is copied into messenger RNA
RNA → Protein (Translation)mRNA is decoded to produce a functional protein
Protein → FunctionProteins carry out biological processes in the cell

Each step in this flow corresponds to a distinct layer of biological information, and each layer can be studied by a different omics discipline. Importantly, bioinformatics provides the tools to analyze data at every single one of these levels.

💡 Note

It is worth noting that the central dogma is not as rigid as it was once thought. Regulatory RNAs, epigenetic modifications, and post-translational protein modifications add additional layers of complexity that bioinformatics must also address.

1.3 The Omics Landscape

The suffix "-omics" refers to the comprehensive study of a class of molecules. Rather than studying individual genes or proteins in isolation, omics approaches aim to measure all molecules of a given type simultaneously. This holistic view is what makes omics data particularly powerful — and particularly challenging to analyze.

Omics DisciplineWhat It MeasuresKey Technique
GenomicsAll DNA sequences in an organismWhole-genome sequencing (WGS), SNP arrays
EpigenomicsChemical modifications of DNA and histones that alter gene expressionWGBS, ChIP-seq, ATAC-seq
TranscriptomicsAll RNA transcripts expressed at a given timeRNA-seq, microarray, single-cell RNA-seq
ProteomicsAll proteins expressed: the proteomeMass spectrometry, 2D gel electrophoresis
MetabolomicsSmall molecule metabolites: the metabolomeLC-MS, GC-MS, NMR spectroscopy
MetagenomicsGenetic material from an entire microbial communityShotgun metagenomics, 16S rRNA sequencing
LipidomicsAll lipid molecules: the lipidomeLC-MS/MS
GlycomicsAll carbohydrates and glycoconjugatesMass spectrometry, lectin arrays

In spaceflight research, transcriptomics and proteomics have been the most widely applied omics disciplines, largely because they capture the dynamic response of cells to their environment rather than just the static genetic blueprint.

1.3.1 Multi-Omics Integration

No single omics layer tells the whole story. A gene may be transcribed into mRNA but the protein may never be made. A protein may be produced but inhibited by a metabolite. Modern spaceflight biology increasingly relies on multi-omics studies that measure several layers simultaneously and integrate them computationally. This concept is explored further in Session 6 on network analysis.

1.4 A Brief History of Bioinformatics

Understanding how bioinformatics developed helps explain both its current capabilities and its limitations.

Era / MilestoneSignificance for Bioinformatics
1953: Watson & Crick describe DNA double helixEstablishes the molecular basis of heredity; creates need for sequence-based analysis
1965: Margaret Dayhoff creates first protein sequence database (PIR)First bioinformatics database; introduces scoring matrices for sequence alignment
1970: Needleman-Wunsch algorithmFirst algorithm for global sequence alignment; foundation of comparative genomics
1977: Sanger sequencing developedMakes DNA sequencing practical; first complete genome (phage φX174) sequenced
1982: GenBank establishedFirst major nucleotide sequence database; international data sharing begins
1990: Human Genome Project begins (completed 2003)13-year, $3 billion project; transforms bioinformatics from niche to essential field
1994: BLAST algorithm publishedRapid sequence similarity search; makes large-scale comparative genomics feasible
2001: First human genome draft published~3.2 billion base pairs; reveals ~20,000 protein-coding genes
2005–2008: Next-generation sequencing platforms emergeCost drops from $1B to <$1K per genome; data generation outpaces computation
2010s: RNA-seq becomes standardReplaces microarrays; enables transcriptome-wide expression profiling
2016: NASA GeneLab launchesFirst comprehensive open omics database specifically for spaceflight experiments
2020s: Single-cell omics, AI/ML integrationCell-level resolution; deep learning transforms sequence analysis and structure prediction

1.5 Why Is Computation Essential?

Consider what happens when an RNA-seq experiment is performed. A tissue sample is collected, RNA is extracted, converted to cDNA, and sequenced. A modern Illumina sequencer can generate over 10 billion base pairs of sequence data in a single run — equivalent to approximately three human genomes. This data arrives as billions of short reads (typically 75–150 nucleotides each), stored in FASTQ format.

To make biological sense of this data, the researcher must:

  • Remove low-quality reads and sequencing artifacts (quality control)
  • Align billions of short reads to a reference genome (alignment)
  • Count how many reads map to each gene (quantification)
  • Statistically test which genes are differentially expressed (statistical modeling)
  • Interpret the results in biological context (downstream analysis)
📊 The Data Challenge of Modern Biology
  • One RNA-seq run: ~50 GB of raw data
  • One whole-genome sequencing run: ~200 GB of raw data
  • GenBank (as of 2024): over 250 billion nucleotide bases in >300 million sequences
  • A typical multi-omics spaceflight study: TB-scale raw data, months of computational analysis

→ No human could analyze this by hand. Algorithms, pipelines, and databases are not optional; they are fundamental to the science.

Key Terms

Bioinformatics
Interdisciplinary field applying computation to biological data analysis
Omics
Comprehensive study of all molecules of a given type (-ome = complete set)
Transcriptome
The complete set of RNA transcripts in a cell at a given moment
FASTQ
File format storing sequencing reads with quality scores
Pipeline
A series of computational steps applied sequentially to process biological data
Algorithm
A step-by-step computational procedure for solving a defined problem

Session Summary

  • Bioinformatics integrates biology, computer science, and statistics to analyze large-scale biological datasets
  • The central dogma (DNA → RNA → Protein) defines the layers of omics data
  • Each omics discipline measures a different molecular layer of biological systems
  • The volume and complexity of modern biological data makes computation non-optional
  • Spaceflight biology extensively uses transcriptomics and proteomics to capture dynamic responses
